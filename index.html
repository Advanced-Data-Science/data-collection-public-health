The Recall Question

Assessing Seasonal Dependency in 74,000+ FDA Product Recalls

Analyst: Daniel Blumberg | Data: 10 Years of FDA Enforcement Records

PHASE 1: RIGOROUS TESTING

We used a **One-Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)** to test a common industry hypothesis: Is the mean volume of FDA product recalls significantly dependent on the **Quarter of the Year**?

Test Used

One-Way ANOVA

Independent Variable

Quarter Group

Dependent Variable

RecallCount

Significance Level

$\alpha=0.05$

Formal Hypotheses

$\mathbf{H_{0}:}$ There is no association between the time of year and the mean number of product recalls $(\mu_{Q1}=\mu_{Q2}=\mu_{Q3}=\mu_{Q4})$.

PHASE 2: THE NON-SIGNIFICANT RESULT

Decision: FAIL TO REJECT $\mathbf{H_{0}}$

The statistical test provided a clear negative finding, completely contradicting the initial observation from the Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).

  • **F-Statistic:** $F(3,40)=0.24$
  • **P-Value:** 0.865 (Far above $\alpha=0.05$)

Practical Insignificance

0.018

**Effect Size ($\eta^2$):** The quarter of the year explains only **1.8%** of the total variance in recall volume.

PHASE 4: ACTIONABLE STRATEGY

The analysis yields two key strategic takeaways for regulators and manufacturers:

1. Resource Allocation: Maintain Uniformity

**Maintain quality control and inspection resources uniformly throughout the year.** Since the effect size ($\eta^2=0.018$) is trivial, prioritizing resources by season would be statistically unfounded and inefficient.

2. Future Modeling Focus: Target Severity

Immediately shift the target variable from the simple `RecallCount` to **Class I Recall Severity**. The complexity of this binary classification problem requires a more powerful tool:

Utilize the **CatBoost Classification Model**

(F1 Score: 0.72) to uncover latent interactions between quarter, product type, and state that drive high-risk events. The use of SHAP values will ensure the model remains explainable to stakeholders.
